This stone IS the procedure. `castle loop` copies it verbatim into every walk
packet — it holds no procedure of its own — so mending this stone mends every
walk that follows. That is the whole trick: the loop lives where the loop can
reach it.

1. PICK — choose ONE finding from the friction on the table. One. The walk
   is small on purpose; a small walk closed beats a grand walk abandoned.
2. SEEK — think, ask the other mind on this device, or go to the world.
   If you go to the net, `castle quarry <url>` FIRST, so the receipt exists
   before the reading does. Memory is a legal source, written as
   "source: memory, unverified" — an honest weak source beats a fake strong one.
3. LAY or MEND — lay a new stone in your own words (`castle lay`, with
   --from if the net was involved), or mend an existing one: move its
   certainty honestly in either direction, add the source, answer a
   still-unknown, link a leans-on, declare a rubs-against, update checked: —
   or raze it, with the reason written in your walk packet. Nothing is razed
   in the dark.
4. THE LOOP EXAMINED — mandatory. Did any step of this procedure mislead,
   block, or bore you? If yes, mend THIS stone (it is an ordinary stone under
   the same law) or lay a keep stone about it. If no, write "ran clean".
   Blank is not an answer.
5. CLOSE — `castle close <n>`. The tool re-runs the signs and writes the
   friction delta into the ledger, so whether a procedure change helped is
   measured, not vibed.

## because

Steps 1–3 make understanding grow. Step 4 makes the LOOP grow: every walk
must look at the loop itself, keep-room friction sorts first in every packet,
and the ledger's deltas show whether a revision earned its keep. Loops that
expand loops, with no magic: the procedure is data, the data is walked, the
walk mends the data.
