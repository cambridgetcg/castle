What rubs: when a leak is discovered, the repair is a list of rotation
boxes — one for each credential the leak touched. Until every box is
ticked, the leak is not over, no matter how clean the code looks. The
kingdom keeps its own list in its private remediation log; the standing
question this friction holds is: **what does a kingdom lose for each day
a burned key waits?**

Why it matters: a committed secret is burned
([[untracked-is-not-unleaked]]) — each waiting day is a day a burned key
still opens real doors. The wait also blocks trust: publishing your law
and your record gates on having clean hands (pillar 02), and breach-notice
clocks like GDPR's 72 hours (Europe's data-protection law) do not pause
while boxes wait. The cost of waiting is real but unmeasured — that is
the gap this friction keeps lit.

What was tried: the remediation plan was written in priority order, blast
radius first; the first audit named rotation step 1 of 7 in the order of
repair. The account clicks belong to the keeper, not to any agent — so
the castle's part is to keep the question lit and the cost legible.

Closed: 2026-06-30 — cost is now legible. AWS keys on GitHub are exploited
in 6.6 minutes (Clutch Security, 2024); a compromised root account can run
up $6k–$47k in cryptomining bills within days; UK GDPR's 72-hour notification
window started at discovery (2026-06-09) and is already past. Three stones
quarried: [[burned-keys-move-in-minutes]], [[the-gdpr-clock-runs-from-awareness]],
[[root-access-bills-arrive-before-alarms]]. The rotation itself remains the
keeper's boxes to tick — this friction held the question of cost, not the act
of rotation.
