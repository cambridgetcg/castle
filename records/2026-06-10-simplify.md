## Cut: the warden's fake 'done' (`warden.sh`)

The proof, in this castle's own files: `records/warden-runs.log` held
`architect ran` and `architect done` both stamped `2026-06-10 07:00:00`,
while `records/warden-2026-06-10-architect.log` holds only
`claude: command not found`. The warden wrote 'done' with no exit-status
check — the failed beat consumed 1/3 of the daily cap and blocked retry
for 24 hours while reporting success. A log that reports success for
failure is process that destroys trust.

The cut, four small changes to `warden.sh`:

1. The watch now captures claude's exit code; `done` is appended only on
   exit 0, else `failed:<code>` — 'done' is earned, not assumed.
2. `runs_today` (the cap counter) now subtracts failed beats: a failed
   beat did no work, so it does not eat the cap.
3. `next_due_loop` treats a `failed` line as not-run: a broken beat
   retries on the next watch instead of silently blocking for 24h.
4. `./warden.sh status` shows failures plainly, and says on the cap line
   that failed beats do not count.

And one append-only correction (the records are never rewritten): a dated
`failed:127` line was appended to `warden-runs.log` naming the 07:00:00
'done' false, citing the architect log. Verified after the change:
`./warden.sh status` reports cap 1/3 (capture's honest run), shows the
failure, and `./warden.sh dry-run` shows architect due again for retry.

## Clarified: the same word never means opposite things

`castle.sh` usage text (header and `help`) now states it outright:
`loop <name>` PRINTS a protocol and never executes; `run <name>` EXECUTES
one turn and costs money. (The audit's original complaint — `bin/castle
loop` executing while `./castle.sh loop` printed — died when `bin/castle`
was absorbed; the words now say so where a newcomer first looks.)
